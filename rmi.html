02 · The backstory

Why Google opened the vault now.

For eighteen years the most useful traffic dataset on the planet powered the blue, yellow, and red lines on a billion smartphones — but never reached the people running the roads themselves. Google Maps users contributed billions of movement signals every day; Google rendered them into colour-coded congestion for drivers; and the agencies responsible for the network couldn't see any of it directly.

That changed on August 25, 2025. Google announced Roads Management Insights, a Google Maps Platform product purpose-built for public-sector road operators. The launch list was small but globally varied — Colorado DOT on the I-70 corridor, the toll operator Abertis in Spain, and the Greek research institute CERTH among the first to switch on. The data is the same data Google has refined for two decades. What's new is that road authorities can now use it directly.

For TraffiCure, that's the foundation we've been waiting for. We've been a Google Maps Platform partner for fifteen years; RMI is the product that lets us bring Google's data to the cities and DOTs we work with, at a scale and price point nothing else in the market can match.

03 · The product, plainly

Three primitives, one pipeline.

RMI is a feed-style product, not a finished application. Three primitives feed one pipeline — and that distinction is the difference between a deployment that ships in eight weeks and one that drifts for eight months.

Scope the network

Hand Google the network you care about — by polygon, GIS layer, or a list of highway designations — and everything downstream is scoped to it. Pricing scales with the size of the selection.

03 · Pub/Sub real-time

Near-real-time speeds

Current speeds per segment, refreshed about every two minutes. Powers live dashboards, incident detection, and dynamic re-routing. Not sub-second — so not for signal control — but fast enough for everything on a screen.

Agencies that self-build the layer above routinely underestimate it — that's four to six months of focused engineering, even with a strong team.

05 · What changes

What RMI changes for the people who run the roads.

It changes what you see.

Most cities saw the 3% of network covered by cameras and maybe 15% by Bluetooth detectors. The other 80%+ was operationally invisible. RMI fills almost all of it, almost immediately, with no hardware to install.

It changes how fast you know.

The old incident workflow — citizen calls, centre logs, unit dispatched, unit reports back — takes 15 to 90 minutes. RMI compresses the first half: the network itself tells you something is wrong in roughly two minutes.

It changes what you can argue for.

Before/after corridor performance becomes legible in days, not quarters. A signal retiming, a one-way conversion, a bus-lane experiment — each one becomes evidence. That changes which projects get funded and which get killed.

It changes what you can answer.

"Is traffic getting better or worse?" Until RMI the honest answer was "we don't know at that granularity." Now it's yes or no — by corridor, by hour, by week. That's a different kind of agency than most departments are used to.

07 · Limits

What RMI can't do — yet, or ever.

We're a Google partner. We're also honest about where the product falls short, because that's what separates pilots that ship from pilots that stall. Four limits matter operationally; the rest are footnotes.

Sparse on quiet roads

Rural segments and low-traffic suburbs thin out, especially overnight. When users on a segment drop below Google's k-anonymity threshold, it goes dark for that interval. Invisible for most cities; real for DOTs covering rural freeways at 2am.

No commercial-only visibility

RMI sees the consumer fleet. Freight-only corridors and private bus lanes under-report. If your decision depends on commercial-vehicle behaviour, pair RMI with INRIX or TomTom Move, both built on fleet GPS.

Not for signal control

The two-minute cadence is fine for dashboards, incident detection, and operator awareness. It is not fine for adaptive signal control or ramp metering. Loop detectors and radar stay in the picture; RMI complements them.

Raw feed only

RMI ships dashboards, alerts, incident correlation, and stakeholder reporting in exactly zero quantity. You build it, buy it, or pick a platform that ships it pre-built — factor four to six months into any self-build estimate.

09 · From the field

What eight months of deployments taught us.

01

The dashboards everyone needs look the same. The logic underneath doesn't.

Across cities of wildly different size, the first three dashboards are nearly identical: live network speed map, incident-feed timeline, "is this worse than yesterday" corridor view. Those are packageable. The alert logic and metrics underneath — which corridors, which thresholds, how alerts route — must be tuned per city. That's the work that turns a generic product into something a city uses daily.

02

Real-time adoption was the surprise.

We expected planners to be the heaviest users. Instead, cities adopt RMI fastest for live operations — for the first time they have a network-wide layer of what's happening right now. That shifts the control room from reactive to proactive. Once a room has watched the network move in real time for a week, going back isn't an option anyone wants.

03

"Before and after" is the highest-value use case nobody talks about.

Every authority has at least one signal retiming or geometric change they argued about for months. Once RMI is live, that argument resolves in a week, with evidence. That single use case has paid for the deployment in three of our four enterprise contracts so far.

04

The biggest May 2026 unlock is simulation.

RMI gave us "what is happening" and "what usually happens." With vehicle counts and the new incident feeds, we can build genuine simulation and forecasting on the same pipeline: capacity scenarios, signal-cycle what-ifs, incident-response predictions, network-wide impact studies. The layer cities have asked for since the beginning — possible the day Google shipped these feeds.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions.

What is Roads Management Insights (RMI)?
A Google Maps Platform product launched on August 25, 2025 that gives public-sector road operators access to anonymized, aggregated traffic data from Google Maps. It delivers historical travel-time patterns and near-real-time speed intervals through BigQuery, Pub/Sub, and the Roads Selection API.
Who is RMI for?
Government road operators: state DOTs, municipal traffic departments, regional planning organizations, toll authorities, and public utilities. It is not designed for private fleets, logistics operators, or consumer apps. Named early adopters include Colorado DOT, Abertis (Spain), and CERTH (Greece).
How does RMI get its data?
It aggregates anonymized movement signals from Google Maps users driving the roads you select. Outputs are speed and travel-time aggregates at the road-segment level, with no personal data exposed. Google applies k-anonymity thresholds before any data leaves the platform.
How often does RMI data refresh?
Near-real-time speed data updates approximately every 2 minutes; historical patterns update on a rolling basis. In our deployments the 2-minute cadence holds consistently during peak hours, with occasional lag on low-volume rural segments.
How is RMI delivered?
Through three channels: the Roads Selection API (define your network), BigQuery tables (historical and batch), and Pub/Sub streams (real-time). It is not a finished dashboard product — agencies build or buy a visualization and decision layer on top.
What's new in RMI as of May 2026?
Three experimental feeds added in the first week of May 2026: anonymized vehicle counts per segment, harsh-braking event clusters at intersection scale, and crowdsourced incident reports from Maps users. All three are flagged experimental; schemas may shift before general availability.
Can RMI vehicle counts replace inductive loop detectors?
For planning, capacity studies, and signal-cycle analysis, RMI counts can replace most loop-detector deployments. For signal-control loops, tolling enforcement, and legally-required calibrated flow measurement, sensors remain necessary. The realistic pattern is hybrid.
What are harsh-braking events used for?
Clusters surface intersections where drivers repeatedly slam on the brakes — a strong proxy for road-design risk. Cities use them for proactive safety remediation, before the matching crashes show up in police data. It's the first sub-network safety signal that needs no new instrumentation.
Are user-reported incidents reliable enough for operations?
Recall is far higher than CCTV-based detection because the user network is millions of times denser. Precision needs light validation; most platforms cross-check against the speed feed. We've found the combined signal more useful than CCTV alone within the first two weeks of any deployment.
How much does RMI cost?
Google has not published public list pricing. It's scoped per agency based on road-network size, refresh frequency, and data volume, negotiated through Google Maps Platform partners. As a 15-year GMP partner and reseller in India and UAE, Lepton can provide a scoped quote on request.
Is RMI available in my country?
It's available globally wherever Google Maps has consumer movement data — most of North America, Europe, the Middle East, and major Asian markets. Density varies by region; urban areas perform strongest. In India and UAE, Lepton sells RMI directly as an authorized reseller.
RMI vs INRIX vs TomTom Move — what's the real difference?
RMI uses Google Maps consumer movement data with deep coverage on consumer-heavy corridors and a ~2-minute refresh. INRIX and TomTom Move use fleet, GPS, and probe data, often stronger on commercial-only routes. RMI is typically more cost-effective for networks over 5,000 km.
Can RMI replace traffic sensors entirely?
For most planning and corridor analytics, RMI can replace or significantly reduce sensor dependency. For signal control, tolling, and legally-required flow measurement, sensors remain necessary.

Is RMI data GDPR-compliant?
RMI outputs contain no personally identifiable information. All data is aggregated at the road-segment level with k-anonymity thresholds applied by Google. The product is designed to meet GDPR, India's DPDP Act, and comparable regimes. Agencies remain data controllers for downstream use; Google is a processor.
